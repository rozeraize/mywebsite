Federal agencies can be created by congress and run unchecked without needing to answer to anyone but themselves. Not only could they make up any regulation they wan't within their sphere of power, but they could decide what falls under there sphere of power. Up until the Chevron doctrine was overthrown, The federal regulatory agency themselves were considered the expert witnesses if anyone tried to dispute the agencies regulation in a court of law. This essentially allowed all federal agencies to make whatever rules they want on a wim. If the subject of the regulation felt that the regulation was unfair and tried bringing it to court, the court could only listen to the regulator as a special witness. With complete unchecked power in their sphere, the ability to decide what falls into that sphere, and no way to dispute their laws, federal regulatory agencies were essentially dictators. This inherently defies the concepts of checks and balances on power and libertarian ideals that this country was built on. This is what the chevron doctrine and auer doctrine allowed. I believe the legislative goverment aught to change the very way it creates federal agencies so that when it creates a federal agency it must first create guidelines and restrictions for that agency instead of leaving it up to the agency to decide the limits of its power on its own.
